Investigating the Space Weather Impact of the 2003 Halloween Geomagnetic Storm by the Ground Magnetic Field Variations: a Global View

Under revision at AGU Space Weather 2024–present
Hongyi Hu (First & Corresponding Author), Zhonghua Xu
Under Review Space Weather

Abstract

Geomagnetic disturbances caused by solar events can have significant impacts on technology on Earth through the creation of geomagnetically induced currents (GICs). This study utilizes the Global-GMD model to perform an error analysis of space weather impacts during the October 29th, 2003, Halloween Storm and evaluate how station spacing affects storm-time mapping accuracy. The analysis focuses on small-scale local geomagnetic disturbances at high-latitude regions, particularly areas like Alaska, where features less than 170 km are evident during periods of heightened geomagnetic activity.

Results indicate that while high-latitude regions experience intense, localized geomagnetic disturbances, the Continental US stations—located at mid-magnetic latitudes with moderate geomagnetic disturbances (200∼400 nT)—exhibit the highest relative errors. This is attributed to limited spatial coverage, with station spacing near BOU around 1000 km, highlighting the importance of enhancing monitoring networks to improve the accuracy of space weather impact assessments.

Key Points

  • Small-scale (<170 km) high-latitude features exist during storms; mapping accuracy depends on these structures and station spacing.
  • CONUS mid-latitude stations (ΔB ∼ 200–400 nT) show highest relative errors due to long baselines (~1000 km) and coverage gaps, not extremes.
  • Recommends ≤150 km spacing in auroral/sub-auroral zones and targeted gap-filling at mid-latitudes (e.g., BOU region).
  • Seven representative stations analyzed across high, mid, and low latitudes: FYU, SMI, B17, BOU, NGK, KAK, PAC.
  • NGK (Europe, dense coverage, 398.7 km avg. spacing) achieves 5.46% median relative error vs. BOU (US, sparse, 1220.6 km) at 38.08%.
  • 2-D magnetometer arrays prove more effective than 1-D arrays for capturing localized storm features.
