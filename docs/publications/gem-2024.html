Global Magnetometer Coverage Efficacy During Geomagnetic Disturbances: A Case Study of the 2003 Halloween Storm

GEM 2024 Workshop July 2024
Hongyi Hu
Presentation Space Weather

Description

Presented at the Geospace Environment Modeling (GEM) 2024 Workshop in Colorado, USA. This talk investigated whether current magnetometer observations provide sufficient spatial coverage for monitoring localized features during geomagnetically disturbed periods, using the first storm period of the 2003 Halloween Storm (06:00–08:00 UTC, Oct 29) as a case study with data from 200+ magnetometer stations globally.

Key Findings

  • Small-scale local features (<100 miles) exist at high-latitude regions like Alaska during highly disturbed periods.
  • Spatial coverage of magnetometer observations is critical for capturing localized features.
  • Best coverage in Europe where relative error is consistently low; Canada and Alaska have slightly worse coverage with error spikes during localized features.
  • Continental US stations (mid-latitude, moderate GMDs of 200–400 nT) show the highest relative errors due to low spatial coverage.
  • Low-latitude regions (Japan, South America) have relatively low errors due to ring current dominance and less localized features.
  • 2-D magnetometer arrays are better than arrays oriented only along longitude or latitude directions.
